# Frameworks for Understanding Ideas

Use these frameworks during the interview and shaping phases to structure thinking. Pick the one that fits the idea — don't force all of them.

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

Focus on the job a customer is hiring your product to do, rather than features. Forces you to articulate the underlying need before defining any solution.

Key question: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

Best for: early-stage ideas where the problem isn't clearly separated from the solution.

## Amazon's Working Backwards (PR/FAQ)

Write a press release and FAQ before building anything. The press release describes the finished product from the customer's perspective; the FAQ anticipates hard questions.

Best for: product ideas that need customer-centricity and stakeholder alignment upfront.

## Shape Up (Basecamp)

Ideas go through a "shaping" phase: define the problem, set an appetite (time/resource budget), sketch rough solutions with explicit boundaries. Output is a "pitch," not a detailed spec.

Best for: ideas where scope control matters — when you need to decide how much time something deserves before designing it.

## OKRs + Opportunity Solution Trees (Teresa Torres)

Pair an outcome (from OKRs) with a visual tree of opportunities, solutions, and experiments. Connects a fuzzy idea back to a measurable business outcome.

Best for: ideas that need to justify their existence against business goals.

## Lean Canvas

Fill in: problem, customer segments, unique value prop, channels, and revenue model. Makes the idea concrete and stress-tested.

Best for: new products or features with unclear market fit.

## The One-Pager / Narrative Memo

A structured 1-2 page narrative covering: problem, customer, proposed solution, success metrics, risks, and open questions. Forces precise thinking.

Best for: any idea that needs to be communicated to others for buy-in.

## CIRCLES Method

Comprehend the situation, Identify the customer, Report their needs, Cut through prioritization, List solutions, Evaluate tradeoffs, Summarize.

Best for: structuring a messy idea into a logical spec flow when you don't know where to start.

## RFC (Request for Comments)

A written document proposing an approach, inviting structured feedback before committing.

Best for: technical or cross-functional ideas that need input from multiple people.

## MoSCoW Prioritization

Must have / Should have / Could have / Won't have. Forces tradeoffs on features once the idea is rough-shaped.

Best for: scoping an idea that's too big — deciding what's in v1 and what isn't.

## Hypothesis-Driven Specs

Frame every idea as: "We believe [doing X] will achieve [outcome Y], and we'll know we're right when [metric Z] changes."

Best for: ideas heavy on assumptions — keeps specs honest about what's proven vs. guessed.
